### 3.2 The pairing TRANSPORT (the net-new piece, #106e-2)

The handshake that gets the new device B's X25519 pairing pubkey to the existing device A so A can `addDevice` + `seal_vdk_to_device`.

- **What the payload encodes.** A fixed-layout, length-checked byte string (NO serde): `DOMAIN || schema_version || vault_id || device_id(32) || x25519_pairing_pub(32) || freshness_nonce`. `device_id` + `x25519_pairing_pub` are exactly the `NewDeviceHandshake` triple #106c already consumes; the `vault_id` binding ties the pairing to the right vault; the freshness nonce defeats replay of a stale QR. This is the `SealedVdkForDevice`-style fixed-layout discipline, applied to a NON-secret payload (the pubkey is public).
- **QR vs short-code (recommend BOTH, distinct roles).** The QR carries the payload BYTES (A scans B's QR â€” the bulk, machine-readable channel). The short-code is the SAS: a short (e.g. 6-digit / 5-word) human-comparable string DERIVED from BOTH devices' pairing pubkeys (+ the freshness nonce), displayed on both screens for the humans to eyeball-compare. They are NOT alternatives â€” the QR moves the pubkey; the SAS authenticates that no MITM swapped it.
- **How the short-code defeats a MITM.** A network/relay MITM that swaps B's pubkey for its own would produce a DIFFERENT SAS on the two devices (the SAS is a function of the actual pubkeys exchanged). The humans compare the codes; a mismatch aborts. This is the standard SAS/numeric-comparison pattern (ZRTP-class) â€” the SAS is a short hash over the transcript of exchanged public keys, surfacing tampering to a human check rather than relying on the channel.
- **How it composes with the chain + seal.** SAS-confirmed â†’ A drives `addDevice` v2 (manager-signed, the on-chain authorization) â†’ A `seal_vdk_to_new_device(vdk, B_triple, vault_id, epoch)` â†’ the `SealedVdkForDevice` travels back to B over the (now SAS-authenticated) channel â†’ B `open_vdk_for_new_device` (the seal is cryptographically bound to B's `device_id` + pubkey, so even if the SAS check were skipped the VDK can only be opened by the real B). The SAS protects the human's trust decision; the seal binding protects the VDK.
- **Where the crypto/encoding lives vs native.** The payload encode/decode + the SAS derive/verify are Rust-side (`pangolin-core::pairing_transport`, exposed over FFI). The camera/QR-scan, the QR rendering, and the screen that shows the SAS for comparison are the native app. Trust boundary: Rust owns the bytes + the SAS math; native owns pixels + the human "do these match?" gesture.

**Q-e â€” the pairing transport: QR, short-code, or both? And what defeats a man-in-the-middle?**
- *Term:* a MITM on the pairing channel could swap the new device's encryption key for its own, then read everything. The defense is a short human-comparable code (a "short authentication string") derived from BOTH devices' keys; if a MITM swapped a key, the codes won't match.
- *Recommendation:* **both, with distinct jobs.** The QR carries the key bytes (the bulk channel A scans); the short-code is the human check both screens show and the two people compare. Belt-and-suspenders: the encrypted VDK is cryptographically locked to the real device anyway, so even a skipped code-compare can't leak the key to a MITM â€” but the code-compare is what stops the human from trusting the wrong device.
- *Plain-English stakes:* this is the security crux of adding a device â€” the moment a stranger could insert themselves. The QR is convenience; the short-code is the actual anti-attacker guarantee. **Stakes: confirm we ship the human-comparable short-code (not QR alone), since QR alone gives a network attacker a window.**

**Q-f â€” the Rust-side vs native-app boundary: where does #106e stop?**
- *Recommendation:* **#106e ships the FFI surface + the pairing-payload encoding + the short-code math (all Rust). The camera/QR-scan, the QR rendering, the device-list/guardian/countdown screens, and the human "do these codes match?" gesture are the native app (Swift/Kotlin), OUTSIDE this repo.**
- *Plain-English stakes:* the repo owns the bytes and the crypto math; the phone app owns the pixels and the camera. **Q-g â€” the guardian roster source for the forward-security re-seal (the #105 Q-f dependency).**

**Q-h â€” the opened-`Share` / pairing-material carrier across FFI (the #105 GAP 3).**
